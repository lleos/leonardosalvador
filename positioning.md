# Portfolio Positioning — Staff Product Designer

This document is evidence-based. Every claim maps to something observable in your career history, the companies you have worked at, or the work visible in the portfolio. Where the evidence is thin, that gap is noted explicitly.

---

## 1. Target Audience

### Primary decision-makers

**VP of Design or CPO at a Series B–D fintech, payment platform, or regulated financial product company.**

These are the people who:
- Have a team of 4–12 product designers and need a senior individual contributor who can operate without constant direction
- Are dealing with product complexity that requires more than UX craft — compliance, risk, multi-market rollout, or platform-scale decisions
- Are not looking for someone to execute tickets, but for someone who can identify the right problem before scoping the solution

### Secondary audience

**Design Directors and Heads of Design at scale-ups in Brazil or LatAm-exposed companies**, particularly those building payments, credit, or financial services infrastructure.

**International companies entering the Brazilian market** who need a designer who understands local payment behavior, regulatory constraints (BACEN, PIX, Open Finance), and the Brazilian user context at first-hand.

### Who this positioning does NOT target

- Early-stage startups that need a generalist designer-writer-researcher
- Companies hiring a design manager (this is an IC positioning)
- Design agencies or consultancies
- Non-financial product domains where the domain expertise does not transfer

## 2. Professional Positioning

**Leonardo Salvador is a Staff-level Product Designer with 15 years of experience, specialized in high-complexity financial products at scale.**

More specifically:
- Deep fintech domain expertise built across two of Brazil's most significant payment and banking companies: Nubank and EBANX

The positioning should lean on the type of work and the complexity of the environment, not the title itself.

## 6. Portfolio Narrative

The portfolio should tell one coherent story across all pages:

**"I design the hard parts of financial products — the experiences where complexity, regulation, and real user behavior have to coexist, at companies that operate at scale."**

This narrative does three things:
1. Self-selects for companies that have hard problems (filters out the easy matches)
2. Names your domain explicitly so there is no ambiguity about fit
3. Implies scale without being self-congratulatory (Nubank, EBANX are well-known enough that the scale is understood)

### What the narrative is not

It is not "I am a strategic partner who cares about business outcomes and user needs." That is every designer's stated positioning. It communicates nothing specific.

It is not "I have 15 years of experience in UX and UI." Length of career is a weak differentiator — what you did with those 15 years is the story.

### How the narrative runs through the site

- **Homepage/masthead**: States the positioning concisely. No "Hey, I'm..." greeting. Opens with context and role, not personality.
- **Case studies**: Each one demonstrates one aspect of the narrative — complexity, regulation, scale, or strategic scope. Not all four at once, but at least one clearly.
- **About page**: Expands the narrative with the trajectory — how 15 years in Brazilian fintech produced the specific expertise.

## 8. Recommended Homepage Messaging

This is not a draft for copywriting. It is a structural recommendation based on what the positioning requires.

### What to replace

Current headline: *"Hey, I'm Leonardo Salvador, a Product Designer solving complex product problems"*

- "Solving complex product problems" is meaningless without context. Every designer says this.
- The greeting is warm but not differentiating. At the Staff level, the portfolio is not selling personality — it is selling expertise and judgment.

### What to say instead

The headline needs to communicate three things in one or two sentences:
1. What you do (design role, not just "designer")
2. Where you do it (fintech/financial products at scale)
3. What makes you different (the specific expertise)

A structural model:
> *[Role description] specializing in [specific domain]. [Evidence of scale or credibility]. [What this means for a hiring company].*

Example structure (not final copy):
> *Product Designer specializing in financial products at scale. 15 years in Brazilian fintech — including Nubank and EBANX — designing complex payment and banking experiences where regulatory constraints, business risk, and user behavior have to coexist.*

The subheading should do what the masthead currently does — name the type of work:
> *End-to-end product design, cross-team systems work, and design strategy for companies building in regulated environments.*

### What not to do

- Do not open with your name in the headline. Your name is in the logo and page title.
- Do not use "passionate about", "love working with", or any similar language.
- Do not list tools (Figma, etc.) anywhere on the homepage.
- Do not describe your process in the masthead — that belongs in the About page and case studies.

## 9. Why You Should Be Considered Staff Level

This is the most important section because it is the claim that requires the most support. The Staff designation means different things at different companies, so this section focuses on the behaviors and scope that define Staff-level work, and whether your career evidence supports each one.

### Staff behaviors and your evidence

**S1. Operates across teams, not within a single squad**

Your About page: *"influencing product decisions across multiple teams and business units."*
Status: *Claimed but not yet demonstrated in case studies.* For this to hold, at least one case study needs to show a decision or initiative that spanned multiple product teams or that set a direction others worked within.

**S2. Identifies the right problem rather than executing a given brief**

Your About page: *"leading and facilitating processes that bring clarity to ambiguous problems."*
Status: *Claimed.* EBANX GO partially demonstrates this — the description mentions navigating constraints rather than executing a spec. Needs more detail in case studies.

**S3. Produces work that other designers build on (systems, standards, principles)**

Status: *Not evidenced in current portfolio.* This is the clearest gap. Staff designers typically have contribution to design systems, shared patterns, or team standards. If this work exists (design tokens, component libraries, interaction guidelines at EBANX or Nubank), it needs to be documented.

**S4. Influences product and roadmap decisions, not just design execution**

Your About page: *"Facilitation of strategic rituals that support roadmap and prioritization"* and *"I act as a strategic partner, not just a delivery role."*
Status: *Claimed.* Needs case study evidence: a specific instance where a design perspective changed a product direction, a roadmap item was added or removed because of design-led research, or a business decision was informed by design's work.

**S5. Operates in high-ambiguity environments without requiring a detailed problem statement**

EBANX GO description: working within regulatory constraints, technical limitations, usability trade-offs simultaneously.
Status: *Partially evidenced.* The EBANX GO description mentions this complexity. The Nubank case study (once written) may show this more clearly given Nubank's organizational scale.

**S6. Company and environment credibility**

Nubank: one of the world's largest neobanks. EBANX: the leading payment technology company for LatAm market access.
Status: *Strong.* These companies set a high bar for product and design quality. Working in them is evidence of operating in a demanding environment.

### The honest gap summary

The core weakness in the current portfolio is that the Staff-level behaviors are stated in the About page but not yet demonstrated in case studies. A hiring committee evaluating a Staff candidate will not take the About page's self-description at face value — they will look for evidence in the case studies. Right now the case studies are images.

The single most important thing to do before positioning as Staff is to write one case study that demonstrates scope beyond a single feature: a project where the decision affected multiple teams, or where design-led insight changed the product direction, or where you set a standard others followed. If that happened at Nubank or EBANX, it needs to be documented. If it cannot be documented (NDA), it still needs to be implied clearly through the way the case study is framed.
